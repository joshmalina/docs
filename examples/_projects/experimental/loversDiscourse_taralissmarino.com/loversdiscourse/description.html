First written in 1977, the premise of Roland Barthes's A Lover's Discourse: Fragments is that the discourse of love is a repository of cultural conventions. The book is structured around 88 entries arranged alphabetically, and these fragments--some of which come from literature and some from Barthes's own philosophical musings--describe how we appear and what we do while in love. Though our own romantic entanglments may feel, above all
else, personal and unique, they are nonetheless understood and expressed in this shared language of love. As Barthes writes, "the amorous subject draws on the reservoir (the thesaurus?) of figures, depending on the needs, the injunctions, or the pleasures of his image-repertoire" (6). The following is reconfigured version of Barthes's collection, drawing randomly from the offerings of Twitter and Google Image Search to bring a Lovers Discourse into the 21st century.
